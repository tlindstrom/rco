很 (hěn) is used to link an adjective with its topic or theme it describes and it has been translated as the degree adverb very a lot. In fact, 很 (hěn) does not necessarily mean very all the time, it sometimes serves as a dummy linking adverb just to link a topic (could be a noun, verb or clause) to an adjective. In this article, we will first look at examples when 很 (hěn) means very and then we will move on to discuss when 很 (hěn) functions as a dummy linking adverb.

很 as very ##

If 很 (hěn) is part of a noun phrase, it functions as the degree adverb very. For example, in 很嚴厲的老師 (hěn yánlì de lǎoshī), 很 (hěn) belongs to the noun phrase 很嚴厲的老師 (hěn yánlì de lǎoshī), it therefore means very. In other words, if 很 (hěn) is used in the following basic structure, it serves as very.
很 + adjective + 的 + noun
For example:
她 是 個 很 勤奮 的 員工 。
tā shì gè hěn qínfèn de yuángōng 。
She is a very hardworking employee.
他 是 個 很 主動 的 學生 。
tā shì gè hěn zhǔdòng de xuéshēng 。
He is a very proactive student.
這 介指 有 很 重要 的 象徵 。
zhè jièzhǐ yǒu hěn zhòngyào de xiàngzhēng 。
This ring has a very important symbol.
這 是 塊 很 苦 的 巧克力 。
zhè shì kuài hěn kǔ de qiǎokèlì 。
This is a very bitter chocolate.
書 裡 有 很多 很 有趣 的 故事 。
shū lǐ yǒu hěnduō hěn yǒuqù de gùshi 。
There are so many very interesting stories in the book.

很 as a dummy linking verb ##

In Chinese, we do not use the verb 是 (shì), meaning to be, to link a topic to its adjective. Instead, we use the negator 不 (bù) and degree adverbs, such as 太 (tài), 非常 (fēicháng) and 十分 (shífēn), etc. to link them. For example:
她 太 友善 了 ！
tā tài yǒushàn le ！
She is too friendly!
這 個 演員 非常 漂亮 。
zhè gè yǎnyuán fēicháng piāoliàng 。
The actress is very beautiful.
這 十分 殘酷 。
zhè shífēn cánkù 。
This is pretty cruel.
駕駛 十分 危險 。
jiàshǐ shífēn wēixiǎn 。
It is dangerous to drive.
他 走路 非常 快 。
tā zǒulù fēicháng kuài 。
He walks very fast.
生 小孩 不 容易 。
shēng xiǎohái bù róngyì 。
It is not easy to give birth to a baby.
So what about if we just want to say A (topic) is B (adjective) without any degree adverbs? We can then use 很 (hěn) to link A to B. 很 (hěn) does not mean very but just functions as a linking adverb without any meaning, which is why we call it 'dummy linking adverb'. In other words, A (topic) + to be + B (adjective) is translated into A (topic) + 很 + B (adjective). 很 (hěn) as a dummy linking adverb bears the following structure:
topic (noun, verb or clause) + 很 + adjective
我 媽媽 很 年輕 。
wǒ māma hěn niánqīng 。
My mother is young.
煮菜 很 容易 。
zhǔcài hěn róngyì 。
Cooking is easy.
睡覺 很 健康 。
shuìjiào hěn jiànkāng 。
Sleep is healthy.
他 平時 說話 很 謹慎 ， 我 也 不 知道 為什麼 他 會 這樣 說 。
tā píngshí shuōhuà hěn jǐnshèn ， wǒ yě bù zhīdào wèishénme tā huì zhèyàng shuō
He is usually careful with his words, I don't know why he would say this either.
Usually when we want to say 'A (topic) is very B (adjective)', we use other degree adverbs such as 太 (tài), 非常 (fēicháng) and 十分 (shífēn) mentioned previously. For example, to express that 'my mother is very young', we could say:
我 媽媽 十分 年輕 。
wǒ māma shífēn niánqīng 。
My mother is very young.

很 (hěn) as very between a noun and an adjective ##

One thing we should note that is that 很 (hěn) does not necessarily serve as a dummy linking adverb in all situations of A (noun) + 很 + B (adjective). Sometimes when we say A (noun) is B (adjective), we are stating a fact instead of a subjective perception. For example, 'glass is transparent' is a fact while 'the glass is very transparent' is a subjective opinion. When such a differentiation exists, 很 (hěn) is only used in the sentence expressing our subjective opinion and has a meaning of very. They have their own sentence structure as well:
To state a fact:
noun + 是 + adjective + 的
To state a subjective opinion:
noun + 很 + adjective
For example:
玻璃 是 透明 的 。
bōlí shì tòumíng de 。
Glass is transparent.
冰 是 硬 的 。
bīng shì yìng de 。
Ice is hard.
這 玻璃 很 透明 。
zhè bōlí hěn tòumíng 。
The glass is very transparent. (could be when to emphasize the glass is so clean that it is very transparent)
這 塊 冰 很 硬 ， 我 咬 不 開 。
zhè kuài bīng hěn yìng ， wǒ yǎo bù kāi 。
The ice is so hard, I can't bite it open. ( to emphasize the ice so hard that I can't bite it open)
