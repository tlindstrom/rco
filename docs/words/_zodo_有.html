有

HSK Level: 1

有 (yǒu) is a common word in Chinese and it is often translated as to have in English. However, to have is only one of its translations, it actually can serve multiple purposes: to express possession, to express existence, to introduce occurrences and to specify quality. In the coming sections, we will discuss each of them with some simple examples.

有 to express possession

When 有 (yǒu) is used to indicate possession, it is correct to translate it into to have. Same as the English equivalent, both animate and inanimate entities could be the subject of 有 (yǒu):
subject + 有 + object

車 有 四 個 車輪 。
chē yǒu sì ge chēlún 。
A vehicle has four wheels.
人類 通常 有 一 對 眼睛 。
rénlèi tōngcháng yǒu yī duì yǎnjīng 。
Humans usually have a pair of eyes.
我 有 個 很 帥 的 兒子 。
wǒ yǒu ge hěn shuài de érzi 。
I have a very handsome son.
書本 應 有 一 個 美麗 的 封面 。
shūběn yīng yǒu yī ge měilí de fēngmiàn 。
Books should have a beautiful cover page.
我 沒 有 經驗 。
wǒ méi yǒu jīngyàn 。
I don't have experience.

(Note that we use 沒 (méi) to negate 有 (yǒu) instead of 不 (bù).)
Sometimes we produce adjectives by putting 有 (yǒu) before certain nominal objects, for example:

這 售貨員 很 有信用 。
zhè shòuhuòyuán hěn yǒuxìnyòng 。
The salesperson is trustworthy. (lit: the salesperson has credit)
總統 很 有錢 。
zǒngtǒng hěn yǒuqián 。
The president is rich. (lit: the president has money)
這 隻 狗 很 有勇氣 。
zhè zhī gǒu hěn yǒuyǒngqì 。
The dog is courageous. (lit: the dog has courage)
為什麼 你 這麼 有把握 你 會 贏 呢 ？
wèishénme nǐ zhème yǒubǎwò nǐ huì yíng ne ？
Why are you so certain that you will win? (lit: why do you have so much certainty that you will win?)

有 to express existence

We also use 有 (yǒu) to indicate the existence of a person or thing at a particular time or place.
place/ time + 有 + noun

昨天 有 一 宗 意外 。
zuótiān yǒu yī zōng yìwài 。
There was an accident yesterday.
街 上 有 很多 車 。
jiē shàng yǒu hěnduō chē 。
There are so many cars on the street.
杯 裡 沒 有 水 。
bēi lǐ méi yǒu shuǐ 。
There isn't water in the glass.
這裡 沒 有 發展 我們 創意 的 機會 。
zhèlǐ méi yǒu fāzhǎn wǒmen chuàngyì de jīhuì 。
There isn't opportunity to develop our creativity.

Note that 有 (yǒu) is used to introduce indefinite nouns only. The examples below sound off as their the nouns following 有 (yǒu) are definite:

門 前 有 那 個 孩子 。
mén qián yǒu nà ge háizi 。
下 星期 有 我的 派對 。
xià xīngqī yǒu wǒ de pàiduì 。
海 裡 有 我的 爸爸 。
hǎi lǐ yǒu wǒ de bàba 。

Instead, 有 (yǒu) in the above examples should be replaced by 是 (shì). 是 (shì) can also function as a existential verb but it can be used to introduce definite as well as indefinite verbs. Click here to learn about the usage of 是 (shì) .

有 to introduce occurrences

If we want to express that an event happens at a particular time or place, 有 (yǒu) could be a useful verb to use. 有 (yǒu) connects a clause with the specific time or place that it takes place at. The subject of the clause must be indefinite in order to form a grammatical sentence with 有 (yǒu) :
time/place + 有 + clause with indefinite subject

昨天 有 個 老師 來 了 我 家 。
zuótiān yǒu ge lǎoshī lái le wǒ jiā 。
A teacher came to my home yesterday.
這裡 有 個 男子 強姦 了 一 個 女人 。
zhèlǐ yǒu ge nánzǐ qiángjiān le yī ge nǚrén 。
A man rapped a woman in here.
上 個 星期 有 老虎 吃 了 人 。
shàng ge xīngqī yǒu lǎohǔ chī le rén 。
A tiger ate a person last week.
公園 裡 有 一 群 學生 在 跳舞 。
gōngyuán lǐ yǒu yī qún xuéshēng zài tiàowǔ 。
A group of students are dancing in the park.

If the place or time is already known in context to the hearer without stating it, or when they are not important or not known to the speaker, they can be omitted.　Therefore, 有 (yǒu) can introduce a clause without mentioning time or place it occurs:
有 + clause with indefinite subject

(世界 上) 有 人 只 有 一 隻 腳 。
shìjiè shàng yǒu rén zhǐ yǒu yī zhī jiǎo 。
(In the world) there are people who only have one leg.

(It is obvious that some people in the universe or world are born with one leg only so it is not necessary to mention where.)

有 人 偷 了 我的 錢包 。
yǒu rén tōu le wǒ de qiánbāo 。
Someone stole my wallet.

(The speaker might not know where or when his/her wallet got stolen.)

有 to express with an adjective how specifically an entity is

有 (yǒu) can be used to specify how an entity is like by stating the degree or extent of its adjective or comparing one entity to another.
When indicating the degree or extent of an adjective, 有 (yǒu) functions to connect a subject and its adjective phrase, giving expressions like how tall or how heavy a person is. 有 (yǒu) must be followed by a numeral which specifies an adjective:
subject + 有 + numeral + adjective
For example:

大樓 有 十 米 高 。
dàlóu yǒu shí mǐ gāo 。
The building is 10 meters tall.
我 有 三十八 公斤 重 。
wǒ yǒu sānshíbā gōngjīn zhòng 。
I weigh 38 kilograms.
路 有 七 公里 長 。
duō yǒu qī gōnglǐ cháng 。
The road is 7 kilometers long.

We can replace the numerals with 多 (duō) to form questions regarding degree or extent:

大樓 有 多 高 ？
dàlóu yǒu duō gāo ？
How tall is the building?
你 有 多 重 ？
nǐ yǒu duō zhòng ？
How much you weigh?
路 有 多 長 ？
lù yǒu duō cháng ？
How long is the road?

As mentioned, 有 (yǒu) can also function to compare one entity to another, making a sentence similar to as...as... We often incorporate 那麼 (nàme) or 那樣 (nàyàng) which mean that or like that in the sentence as well. Its structural formula is as the following:
entity A + 有 + entity B + (那麼/那樣) + adjective
For example:

大樓 有 這 座 山 (那樣) 高 。
dàlóu yǒu zhè zuò shān nàyàng gāo 。
The building is as tall as this mountain.
我 有 你 (那麼) 重 。
wǒ yǒu nǐ nàme zhòng 。
I weigh as much as you do.
路 沒 有 長城 (那麼) 長 。
lù méi yǒu chángchéng nàme cháng 。
The road isn't as long as The Great Wall.
