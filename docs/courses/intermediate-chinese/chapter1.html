Parallel Text: What do you like to do?

What is it for?

A：你這個週末有空嗎？ A: Are you free this weekend?
Ｂ：有，為什麼呢？ B: Yes, why?
Ａ：你要一起去打籃球嗎？ A: Do you want to play basketball together?
我上個星期踢足球的時候傷了我的腳，現在不能打籃球。你喜歡游泳嗎？ I hurt my foot last week while playing football, so I can't play basketball now. Do you like to swim?
我不會游泳！ A: I can't swim!
Ｂ：我可以教你。 B: I can teach you.
Ａ：我怕水！我們可以一起去看電影。 A: I am afraid of water. We can go watch a movie together.
Ｂ：我不喜歡去電影院看電影。你要一起逛街嗎？我們附近開了一間很大的購物商場。 B: I don't like watching movies in a cinema. Do you want to go shopping together? There is a newly open shopping mall near by us.
Ａ：是嗎？但是我沒有什麼要買，而且週末商場有很多人。 A: Really? But I have nothing to buy, and there will be many people in the shopping malls during weekend.
Ｂ：那你平時喜歡做什麼呢？ B: So what do you usually like to do?
Ａ：我喜歡做運動，除了籃球之外，我也喜歡打羽毛球，打排球和跑步。我每天晚上都會看書和聽古典音樂。週末我經常去看電影，有時候會去遠足。你呢？ A: I like to do sports. Besides playing basketball, I also like playing badminton, volleyball and running. I read and listen to classic music every night. During weekend I usually go to watch a movie, sometimes I will go hiking. What about you?
Ｂ：我很少看書，因為看書很悶！我也喜歡做運動，我每個週末都會跟鄰居踢足球，而且每天放學後都會去跳芭蕾舞，或練習瑜伽。 B: I rarely read, because reading is boring! I also like doing sports, I play football with my neighbors every weekend. I dance ballet every day after school, or practise yoga.
Ａ：我住在廣州的時候也喜歡跟鄰居踢足球，但現在我不認識我的鄰居。 A: I liked to play football with my neighbors while I was living in Guangzhou, but now I don't know my neighbors.
Ｂ：我初搬進來的時候也不認識我的鄰居，但現在我們是好朋友。 B: I didn't know my neighbors when I just moved into here either, but now we are good friends.
Ａ：你總是能結交新朋友！ A: You can always make new friends!
Ｂ：他們這個週末會舉辦烤肉聚會，你要來嗎？ B: They are hosting a barbecue party this weekend, do you want to join?
Ａ：好啊！ A: Yes!

Grammar

我 I, me
你 you
您 you (respectful form)
他 he, him
她 she, her
它 it (for non-living objects)
牠 it (for animals)

Unlike in English, we do not make changes on words for tenses, plurality or cases. This is why some people say that Chinese has no grammar. In Chinese, '他' can be ‘he’ or ‘him’ in English. When it comes to plural forms, we simply attached 們 (men) to the pronouns we want to pluralize. Therefore, the plural forms of the pronouns in the above are as in the following:

我們 we, us
你們 you (plural)
您們 you (plural, respectful form)
他們 they, them (masculine)
她們 they, them (feminine)
它們 they, them (for non-living objects)
牠們 they, them (for animals)

As you can see, Chinese does have grammar, but just appears in a different form.

Word Order in Chinese

The basic word order in Chinese is Subject + Predicate. A predicate is usually a verb, an adjective or a noun phrase. For example:

　　　 　　　 　　　 　　　
Subject Predicate
我 (I) 愛 (to love) 你 (you)
你 (You) 吃 (to go to) 飯 (rice)
它們 (They) 是 (to be) 玩具 (toy)
他(He) 很 (very) 醜 (ugly)
她(She) 不(not) 美(beautiful)
昨天 (Yesterday) 中秋節 (mid-autumn Festival)
我(I) 三十歲 (thirty years old)

Note that changing orders of words in a sentence results in a change of meaning. For example, 我愛你(wǒ ài nǐ), which means _I love you_, while 你愛我 (nǐ ài wǒ) means _you love me_.

是 (to be)

In general, 是 (shì) is used to introduce a predicative which is used to give further explanation or description of a topic. Unlike to be in English, 是 (shì) can only be followed by a noun/pronoun phrase. Therefore, 是 (shì) can function as the verb to be as in she is a girl but not she is beautiful or she is there. In other words, 是 (shì) is used to connect two nouns/pronouns as in the following:

小籠包是上海菜。
Xiaolongbao is a Shanghainese dish.

他是華人。
He is a Chinese.

她是我的姐姐。
She is my sister.

這個杯是我的禮物。
The cup is my gift.

你是壞人。
You are a bad person.

To negate 是 (shì), we simply put the negator 不 (bù) before it. For example:

他不是華人。
He is not a Chinese.

她不是我的姐姐。
She is not my sister.

你不是壞人。
You are not a bad person.

At this level, you only need to learn the usage of 是 as ‘to be’. As you proceed to a higher level, you will understand 是 is more than just ‘to be’. To learn more about the comprehensive usage of 是, please click [here].
