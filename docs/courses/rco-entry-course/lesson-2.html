Chapter 2 - Introduction to Chinese Grammar

Statements

We saw this sentence in the previous chapter:

我 喜歡 你 。
Wǒ xǐhuān nǐ.
I like you.

In this case, Chinese grammar is just like English. Now let's turn this sentence around:

你 喜歡 我 。
Nǐ xǐhuān wǒ.
You like me.

The person at the beginning of the phrase is the subject, and that is the one doing the liking. Likewise, the person at the end of the phrase is the object, and that is the one who is liked by the subject. From this you can see that the most basic sentence structure of Chinese is very similar to English.

Of course, you can like things and actions too, not just people:

我 喜歡 貓 。
Wǒ xǐhuān māo.
I like cats.
你 喜歡 看 書 。
Nǐ xǐhuān wǒ.
You like me.

As you can see, 貓 is the Chinese word for cats. But be aware that 貓 also means cat, singular. It can even also mean the cat, and the cats. In English, you always explicitly state whether a noun is singular or plural, definite or indefinite. However, in Chinese this is often left up to context.

Yes-No Questions

Asking yes-no questions in Chinese is incredibly easy. All you do is make a statement . . .

你 喜歡 貓 . . .
Nǐ xǐhuān māo . . .
You like cats . . .

And then add the question particle 嗎 to the end:

你 喜歡 貓 嗎 ？
Nǐ xǐhuān māo ma?
Do you like cats?

In order to answer yes, you merely repeat the verb:

喜歡 ！
Xǐhuān!
Yes! (Like!)

In order to answer no, you add 不 to the verb:

不 喜歡 ！
Bù xǐhuān!
No! (Not like!)

Combining Sentences into One

Just like in English, we can take two sentences and combine them into one with words like “but”, which is 但是 in Chinese.

Let’s look at this conversation I had with Phil about cats:

我 ： Phil ， 我 喜歡 貓 。 你 喜歡 貓 嗎 ？
Wǒ: Phil, wǒ xǐhuān māo. Nǐ xǐhuān māo ma?
Me: Phil, I like cats. Do you like cats?
Phil ： 不 喜歡
Phil: Bù xǐhuān!
Phil: No!
我 ： 喔 ， 我 喜歡 貓 ， 但是 你 不 喜歡 貓 。
Wǒ: Ō, wǒ xǐhuān māo, dànshì nǐ bù xǐhuān māo.
Me: Oh, I like cats, but you don't.
