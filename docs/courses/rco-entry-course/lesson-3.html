Chapter 3 - Building Sentences, Part 1: Personal Pronouns

You and I - 你 and 我

Let's talk about you, me, and some animals, like 馬 and 鱷魚

你 喜歡 馬 。
Nǐ xǐhuān mǎ.
You like horses.
我 喜歡 鱷魚 。
Wǒ xǐhuān èyú.
I like crocodiles.

Let’s flip these around like we did in Chapter 2. This time, pay attention to what happens to the English pronouns versus the Chinese pronouns.

馬 喜歡 你 。
Mǎ xǐhuān nǐ.
Horses like you.
鱷魚 喜歡 我 。
Èyú xǐhuān wǒ.
Crocodiles like me.

Note that 我 means both "I" and "me". While personal pronouns in English have separate subject and object forms (like "I" and "me"), Chinese personal pronouns just have one form.

He, She - 他, 她

Next, we have 他 for "he" and "him", and 她 for "she" and "her". In spoken Chinese, these words are all pronounced the same way, regardless of whether they're referring to male or female.

她 喜歡 吃 芒果 。
Tā xǐhuān chī mángguǒ.
She likes to eat mangoes.
他 喜歡 吃 葡萄 。
Tā xǐhuān chī pútáo.
He likes to eat grapes.
鱷魚 想 吃 他 。
Èyú xiǎng chī tā.
The crocodile wants to eat him.
馬 想 吃 她 。
Mǎ xiǎng chī tā.
The horse wants to eat her.

At first glance, 他 and 她 look a bit similar, but it’s easy to tell them apart if you break the characters down into their radicals, which are the basic building blocks of chinese characters.

On the left one, we see 亻, which is a squashed version of 人,, which means person or mankind. Paired with 也,, it becomes 他- - the male pronoun. 女 is the female radical. Paired with 也, it becomes 她 - the female pronoun.

It - 牠 and 它

There are two terms for “it”. They are 牠 and 它. They share the same pronunciation as 他 and 她。

牠 is used exclusively for animals.

我 想 看 鱷魚 。
Wǒ xiǎng kàn èyú.
I want to look at the crocodile.
牠 想 吃 我 。
Tā xiǎng chī wǒ.
It wants to eat me.

它 is used more generally for things, be they furniture, food, OR animals.

蛋糕 ！ 我 想 吃 它 ！
Dàngāo! Wǒ xiǎng chī tā!
Cake ! I want to eat it!
椅子 ！ 我 不 想 吃 它 ！
Yǐzǐ! Wǒ bù xiǎng chī tā!
A chair! I don’t want to eat it!

Plural Pronouns - 們

Instead of using a bunch of different words for plurals like they, them, we, or us, Chinese simply uses the character 們 to make any pronoun plural.

我 喜歡 他們 ．
Wǒ xǐhuān tāmén.
I like them.
我們 不 喜歡 看 鱷魚 。
Wǒmén bù xǐhuān kàn èyú.
We don't like to look at crocodiles.
牠們 想 吃 我們 ．
Tāmén xiǎng chī wǒmén.
They want to eat us.
你們 喜歡 吃 芒果 。
Nǐmén xǐhuān chī mángguǒ.
You like to eat mangoes.

Inclusive and Exclusive “We”

Lastly, there are two different ways to use “we” in Chinese - exclusive and inclusive. 我們 is the exclusive version. Imagine you’re talking to Phil about how you and some friends will eat cake.

Phil ， 我們 會 去 吃 蛋糕 。
Phil, wǒmén huì qù chī dàngāo
Phil, we will go eat cake.

Do you think Phil is included in these cake-eating plans? Nope! In Chinese 我們 is an exclusive we. That is, it doesn’t include the person being spoken to in the “we.” I’m simply rubbing in Phil’s face that my friends and I will soon be eating cake, but he won’t.

Chinese has an inclusive we that includes the person being spoken to. This word is 咱們.

Phil ， 咱們 都 會 去 吃 蛋糕 ！
Phil, zánmén dōu huì qù chī dàngāo!
Phil, we’re all going to go eat cake!

This time, Phil is enthusiastically invited along to eat cake with my other friends and me.

Parallel Text Practice

Here are a few sentences to let you practice what you’ve learned. Try focusing on just the Chinese before looking over at the English!

你 想 吃 葡萄 ， 但是 我 不 喜歡 吃 葡萄 。 我 喜歡 吃 芒果 ！ You want to eat grapes, But I don't like to eat grapes I like to eat mangoes!
你 想 看 鱷魚 ， 但是 Phil 不 想 看 牠們 。 You want to look at crocodiles, but Phil doesn’t want to look at them.
他 想 看 貓 ， 但是 貓 不 喜歡 他 。 牠們 喜歡 你 嗎 ？ He wants to look at cats, but cats don’t like him. Do they like you?
